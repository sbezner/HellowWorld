Walnut Mountain Shelter

NC/TN Border  ·  AT Mile ~262.1  ·  Lean-to (CCC 1938)  ·  Elev. ~4,260 ft

Unreliable Water Source: The spring at Walnut Mountain is notoriously unreliable, especially during dry periods. The blue-blazed trail to the spring can be difficult to locate. Carry water from Lemon Gap or a reliable source before arriving. A better campsite with stream water is located at the gap 0.75 miles north of the shelter.

Walnut Mountain Shelter is a historic log lean-to built by the Civilian Conservation Corps in 1938, making it one of the oldest shelters still in use on the Appalachian Trail. The small structure sleeps 5 under a tin roof at 4,260 feet on the NC/TN border. Despite its age, the shelter remains functional — rustic and weathered but standing strong after nearly 90 years.

The shelter's most significant challenge is its unreliable water source. The Walnut Creek Spring is accessed via a blue-blazed trail that can be difficult to locate, especially in leaf-on season. The spring frequently dries up during summer and fall dry spells. Experienced hikers carry water from Lemon Gap (about 10 miles south) or plan to continue 0.75 miles north to a gap with a more reliable stream and good tent sites.

At 4,260 feet, Walnut Mountain sees cold temperatures even in summer — pack accordingly if staying overnight. The surrounding forest is a mature hardwood mix of oak, beech, and birch. Bear activity is common between Max Patch and Garenflo Gap, so use the bear cables provided. The terrain ahead descends gradually toward Hot Springs over the next 12 miles.

Historic Shelter: The CCC (Civilian Conservation Corps) built hundreds of structures across the Appalachian Trail in the 1930s. Walnut Mountain Shelter is one of the few surviving original CCC shelters still serving hikers. The hand-hewn log construction and tin roof are a living link to the trail's earliest days.

Getting Supplies

Hot Springs is just 12 miles north — an easy half-day hike. Most hikers push through without stopping.

RECOMMENDED

Hot Springs, NC — ~12 trail miles north

~12 trail miles north, mostly downhill. The trail descends from Walnut Mountain's ridgeline toward the French Broad River and enters Hot Springs from the south. The AT walks right through town. Full resupply at Dollar General and convenience stores, restaurants, outfitter, post office, and the mineral hot springs spa. A comfortable half-day hike from Walnut Mountain.

ROAD ACCESS

Lemon Gap — ~10 trail miles south

~10 trail miles south on the AT. Lemon Gap (NC-1182/TN-107) is a paved road crossing with no services. Del Rio, TN is reachable via TN-107 but hitchhiking is difficult due to low traffic. Only viable as an emergency exit.
