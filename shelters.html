Appalachian Trail Shelters

1. Blood Mountain Shelter

Georgia — Mile 31.7 (NOBO)

One of the oldest and most iconic shelters on the AT, Blood Mountain is a two-room stone cabin built by the Civilian Conservation Corps in the 1930s. Sitting at 4,461 ft — the highest point in Georgia on the trail — it offers sweeping views and a real sense of history. No water on-site; the nearest source is 0.5 miles away.

2. Roan High Knob Shelter

Tennessee/North Carolina border — Mile 380

At 6,285 ft, this is the highest shelter on the entire Appalachian Trail. Enclosed and heated by its stone walls, it's a welcome refuge given the exposed alpine conditions on Roan Mountain. The surrounding Rhododendron Gardens bloom brilliantly in June, making it a favorite destination for hikers and day visitors alike.

3. The Priest Shelter

Virginia — Mile 851

Famous among thru-hikers for its "confession log" — a tradition where hikers write humorous confessions of trail sins like skipping miles or eating too much town food. Perched near the summit of The Priest at 4,063 ft in the Blue Ridge Mountains, it also rewards visitors with outstanding views of the Tye River Valley below.

4. Annapolis Rock Shelter

Maryland — Mile 1,024

A popular weekend destination for hikers from Washington D.C. and Baltimore, Annapolis Rock sits atop a dramatic quartzite outcrop overlooking the Cumberland Valley. The shelter itself is clean and well-maintained, with a reliable water source nearby. Sunsets from the rocks are spectacular and draw crowds on clear evenings.

5. Horns Pond Shelter

Maine — Mile 2,063

Set beside a pristine alpine pond on the Bigelow Range, Horns Pond is one of Maine's most beautiful campsites. Two lean-tos sit at the water's edge with views of the Horns peaks reflected in the still water. Moose sightings are common at dawn. It's a fitting reward for hikers nearing the end of their journey to Katahdin.
